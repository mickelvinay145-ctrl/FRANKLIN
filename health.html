Daily responsibility of the aquarists

Observation of the enclosure is crucial to ensure that the rockhoppers under captivity are safe and healthy by monitoring their activity, identifying potential diseases, and evaluating nutritional needs. As reported by Maganhe (2024), effective monitoring can help track changes in eating habits and unusual behavioural patterns. Any abnormality in feeding behaviour is usually associated with the health status of the captive birds. Monitoring helps to detect any significant information about potential nutritional problems, stress, poor food, and possible disease risks. Aquarists have to learn the usual behaviours exhibited by penguins, such as physical activities, swimming, posture, and interaction, to notice any behavioural changes (San Diego Zoo, 2023). The cleaning process of the cage on a daily basis is essential in helping to prevent the development of both fungal and bacterial diseases, since the failure to clean leads to a high risk of parasitic infection (Itoh et al., 2023). The examination of faeces can help to identify any digestive problems or potential infections by assessing the state of the faecal material (Kim et al., 2024). Cleaning and sanitisation of the cages housing penguins is an important aspect, as it helps to get rid of any pathogens within the surrounding environment.

Weekly/Monthly monitoring behaviour

Periodic monitoring of health, in terms of weekly or monthly check-ups, is necessary to understand the long-term requirements of health for the captive members. Routine weight checks help to check the conditions of the body to point out early signs of weakness and environmental issues (Leineweber et al., 2023). Body weight checking and condition scoring are the standard requirements for behavioural pattern monitoring. Water quality checking and ensuring cleaning of the pools are additional requirements under weekly monitoring plans (Leineweber et al., 2023). The likelihood of toxic compounds in the water should be monitored thoroughly, including tests such as pH, nitrite, and nitrate tests (Kim et al., 2024). Similarly, Itoh et al. (2023) have proposed the significance of bacterial testing to check the total bacterial count in the enclosure and the detection of fungi such as Aspergillus sp. To improve the mortality rate of the captive birds. Additionally, environmental monitoring is needed to ensure optimal conditions in terms of water temperature, turbidity, and salinity levels. The quality of water must be monitored effectively to prevent illness, feather damage, and the overall well-being of the captive penguins.

Health Management and Intervention Strategies in a Captive Environment

Captive rockhopper penguins have a susceptibility to infectious diseases because of the small confinement space. Infectious diseases may affect the population within a short while through indirect or direct contact with the colony through food, water, and nests. Diseases such as aspergillosis and avian malaria are some examples of infectious diseases that affect mortality levels among captive penguins (Stidworthy & Denk, 2018). Avian malaria, which is caused by the parasite Plasmodium sp., affects captive penguins. In captive colonies, avian malaria leads to deaths that range from 60% to 80%. Aspergillosis is a deadly respiratory disease that affects captive penguins caused by Aspergillus sp. fungi, which are widely available across water and soil.
